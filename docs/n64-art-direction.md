# Look Over Here — N64 Art Direction

## Core Visual Target

**A lost N64 exploration game from an alternate timeline: chunky, foggy, readable, strange, funny, and full of big silhouettes.**

Not realistic.

Not modern sci-fi.

Not polished Pixar.

Not generic low-poly.

It should feel like:

> “This place is simple enough to understand, but weird enough that I want to walk toward it.”

## Style Summary

Look Over Here should be **first-person, chunky low-poly 3D exploration** with a strong N64-inspired identity.

The visual style should support one player seeing something in the distance and saying:

> “Look over here.”

## Visual Pillars

### 1. Big Silhouettes First

Every important thing should be recognizable from far away.

Examples:

- giant fossil spine
- floating rock arch
- dead robot hand
- mushroom tower
- impossible cube
- crooked moon elevator
- sleeping creature ridge
- huge sealed egg

If players cannot describe where they are to each other, the art failed.

### 2. Fog Is a Feature

N64-style fog should be part of the mood and production strategy.

Fog helps:

- create mystery
- hide map edges
- reduce detail burden
- make landmarks appear dramatically
- keep procedural worlds readable
- make small worlds feel larger

The fog should feel atmospheric, not like a technical limitation.

### 3. Chunky Geometry

Use simple shapes with personality:

- low-poly terrain
- angular rocks
- oversized plants
- blocky ruins
- weird machines
- thick doors
- exaggerated caves
- simple creature bodies

Avoid tiny detail.

Use shape, color, and placement.

### 4. Limited Texture Language

Textures should be simple and bold.

Good options:

- flat colors
- vertex colors
- chunky gradients
- simple repeating patterns
- pixelated decals
- low-resolution signs/symbols
- simple hand-painted masks

Avoid detailed realistic surfaces.

### 5. Readable Weirdness

The world can be strange, but players need to know what matters.

Use consistent visual signals:

- traversal objects stand out
- goal objects feel special
- dangerous things warn the player
- scannable things have a subtle pulse
- secrets have visual tells
- interactable objects have recognizable shape language

Weird should invite curiosity, not confusion.

### 6. Toy-Scale Alien Worlds

The planets should feel like small explorable dioramas, not huge realistic planets.

A good area feels like:

> “A strange little place I can understand, explore, and remember.”

The map can be small if the landmarks are strong.

## Camera Direction

Start with **first-person 3D**.

Why:

- strongest exploration feeling
- easiest way to create scale and mystery
- supports looking up, peeking around corners, scanning, climbing, and pointing
- makes co-op discoveries feel personal
- avoids early third-person animation and camera complexity

Visible hands/tools can come later if needed.

## Fidelity Target

Aim for:

# N64-plus, not modern open world.

That means:

- simple models
- bold shapes
- chunky terrain
- atmospheric fog
- limited texture detail
- strong lighting mood
- readable object colors
- stylized props
- intentionally low-detail forms

The game should look intentional, not unfinished.

## Reference Feel

Use these as taste references, not direct copies:

- **The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time / Majora’s Mask** — readable 3D adventure spaces and landmarks
- **Super Mario 64** — simple shapes and toy-like worlds
- **Banjo-Kazooie** — chunky prop personality
- **Star Fox 64** — angular sci-fi forms
- **Journey to the Savage Planet** — colorful alien comedy and scanning/discovery
- **Outer Wilds** — curiosity, landmarks, and world rules

## Prototype Biome

# Forbidden Moon

The first art target should be one excellent N64-inspired alien biome.

Visual kit:

- crater terrain
- heavy colored fog
- low-poly cliffs
- giant fossil bones
- glowing mushrooms
- floating rocks
- one weird machine set
- one simple creature silhouette
- one impossible landmark visible from spawn

The player should spawn and immediately see at least one thing that makes them say:

> “Look over here.”

## Landmark Formula

Each generated world should have:

### 1. One Dominant Landmark

Visible almost immediately.

Examples:

- giant fossil spine
- dead robot hand
- floating rock arch
- glowing mushroom tower
- broken moon elevator
- huge egg in a crater

### 2. Two Secondary Curiosities

Smaller places players want to inspect.

Examples:

- smoking cave
- weird tree circle
- abandoned scanner station
- tiny shrine
- animal nest
- half-buried arcade machine

### 3. One Impossible-Looking Thing

Something that makes the player ask:

> “Can we get there?”

Examples:

- island floating above the map
- door inside a cliff face
- bridge made of sleeping animals
- tower upside down
- light beam hitting nothing
- cave mouth that opens and closes

## Palette Direction

Use strong biome palettes rather than realistic color.

Examples:

### Forbidden Moon

- black rock
- blue slime
- pale yellow fog
- pink sky

### Rotting Forest

- dark green
- orange fungus
- bone-white trees
- purple shadows

### Dead Arcade

- black glass
- neon signs
- red carpet dirt
- glowing cabinet portals

### Polygon Desert

- tan sand
- teal ruins
- red sun
- black fog

### Giant’s Garden

- oversized grass
- pastel flowers
- huge bones
- soft sky

Each world should be identifiable from screenshots.

## Interaction Readability

Because the game is procedural, players need visual language.

Possible signal system:

- **Blue glow** = traversal
- **Gold glow** = goal/objective
- **Green pulse** = living or scannable
- **Red/orange flicker** = danger or instability
- **White sparkle/outline** = carryable or usable object

These exact colors can change later.

The rule matters:

> Weird does not mean unreadable.

## Things to Avoid

Avoid starting with:

- realistic terrain
- detailed character models
- complex animation
- huge biomes
- survival-game visual clutter
- procedural textures
- high-poly alien fauna
- expensive water/foliage systems
- cinematic lighting dependency
- generic asset-store sci-fi
- many art styles at once

## Producer Rule

Do not build many styles yet.

Start with one excellent N64-inspired alien biome.

If that works, expand later.

The first art goal is not beauty.

It is:

> **Can two players navigate by landmarks and feel curious?**

# Look Over Here — N64 Art Reference

## Status

This document replaces the old idea that Look Over Here should be a strict N64-style game.

N64 is now a **reference**, not the art target.

The current art target is defined in:

- `scenic-low-poly-art-direction.md`

## What To Keep From N64 Inspiration

N64-era games are still useful because they understood readability under constraint.

Keep these lessons:

### Big Silhouettes

Important things should be recognizable from far away.

Examples:

- fossil spine
- giant arch
- mountain hole
- huge tree
- signal tower
- strange creature ridge

### Simple Shapes

Use forms players can read quickly:

- chunky cliffs
- angular rocks
- simplified plants
- bold platforms
- thick doors
- clean landmarks

### Strong Landmark Composition

The player should be able to describe locations easily:

> “Go to the big yellow spine.”

> “Meet me under the arch.”

> “I’m by the glowing tree.”

### Low Detail Density

Avoid tiny visual noise.

Use:

- large color fields
- simple material zones
- clear object categories
- bold contrasts
- readable trails

### Charm

The world can be strange, funny, and toy-like without becoming realistic.

## What Not To Keep

Do not keep N64 limitations that fight the new project direction.

Avoid:

- heavy fog walls
- tiny draw distance
- cramped spaces only
- low visual ambition
- pretending technical limits are style
- making the game look old for its own sake

## How To Use This Reference

When implementing art, ask:

- Is the silhouette readable?
- Is the path clear?
- Can players describe the landmark?
- Is the scene simple enough to understand?
- Does the low-poly form have personality?

Then also ask the new scenic questions:

- Is there a breathtaking view?
- Can players see far away?
- Does height reveal something?
- Does the world feel like an impossible national park?
- Is there a distant curiosity worth walking toward?

The scenic direction wins if these conflict.

# Look Over Here — Scenic Low-Poly Art Direction

## Core Visual Target

**Look Over Here should look like a playable gouache landscape painting with readable trails, tall climbs, huge views, strange distant landmarks, and saturated dreamlike color.**

The game should feel like visiting impossible national parks from another universe.

Not strict N64.

Not realistic.

Not generic low-poly.

Not empty alien planet terrain.

It should feel like:

> “This place is beautiful. I want to climb higher so I can see what that is.”

## Visual Identity

### Current Art Target

**Playable Gouache Landscape Painting.**

Low-poly is an optional construction method, not the visual goal. Use simple 3D
forms where they help readability, but the visible result should be painterly,
atmospheric, and scenic—not a display of flat-shaded geometry. The world should
feel like a gouache landscape painting the player can walk into.

The target composition is:

- foreground overlook
- valley or cloud sea below
- one dominant hero mountain
- layered distant mountain silhouettes
- huge sky and atmospheric depth

At a squint, the opening view should resolve into four large painted regions:
foreground ridge, cloud-filled valley, hero mountain, and sky. Prefer broad
landscape masses, atmospheric overlap, and painterly color blocking over
separate cones, towers, cloud puffs, pads, or foreground prop clusters.

Cloud seas and hero mountains are composition anchors. They should create scale
before individual gameplay objects become readable.

Prioritize soft sky gradients, horizon haze, cool distance fading, warm-light
and cool-shadow separation, and large connected color regions. Avoid raw
flat-shaded toy-prop scenery even when the underlying geometry is low-poly.

### The Target

**Painted impossible national park dreamscape.**

The world uses recognizable outdoor beauty:

- canyons
- mountains
- rivers
- waterfalls
- forests
- ridgelines
- arches
- mesas
- alpine meadows
- high trails
- scenic overlooks

Then bends it through:

- impossible geology
- saturated dream color
- moving curiosities
- strange weather
- giant natural landmarks
- playful alien details
- simple readable low-poly forms
- fewer foreground props
- more negative space for the vista to breathe
- cloud banks that act as scale and depth forms, not decorative puffs
- one dominant hero mountain instead of many equal-sized rock towers

## N64 Is Inspiration, Not Target

N64 can still inspire:

- chunky shapes
- readable silhouettes
- simple landmarks
- bold color blocking
- charming abstraction
- low detail density

But N64 should not dictate:

- short draw distance
- heavy fog walls
- low visual ambition
- tiny enclosed crater spaces
- strict retro technical limits

The current direction wants scenic payoff first, with N64 only as a readability reference.

## Visual Pillars

### 1. Scenic First

Every major world should have at least one postcard view.

A player should be able to stop, look out, and feel:

> “Wow, we should go there.”

The vista is not background decoration. It is motivation.

### 2. Trails Create Desire

Trails should visibly lead somewhere.

A trail can be:

- dirt path
- ridge line
- riverbed
- root bridge
- bone spine
- cloud stair
- glowing stone path
- animal track
- canyon switchback

The player should read the world and think:

> “That path probably goes somewhere.”

### 3. Height Creates Reward

Climbing is a major emotional and mechanical reward.

High points should reveal:

- the objective
- distant landmarks
- shortcuts
- strange movement
- hidden routes
- the true scale of the place

Avoid flat maps where height only exists as decoration.

### 4. Long Views, Not Fog Walls

Use atmosphere to create depth, not to hide everything.

Good atmosphere:

- distant haze
- gradient sky
- atmospheric perspective
- soft color falloff
- cloud layers
- sun shafts later
- light mist in valleys

Bad atmosphere:

- opaque fog wall that makes the world feel tiny
- hiding all distant landmarks
- making the level feel like an empty test arena

### 5. Low-Poly Forms, Modern Feeling

Keep geometry simple and readable.

Use:

- chunky cliffs
- simplified trees
- bold mountains
- angular rocks
- clean silhouettes
- stylized props
- readable platform surfaces

But allow modern techniques if lightweight:

- long draw distance
- shader gradients
- atmospheric haze
- simple water shaders
- moving clouds
- vertex color blending
- stylized lighting
- animated grass/foliage later

### 6. Earth Structure, Dream Color

Use real outdoor places as the base.

Then stylize the palette.

Examples:

- peach canyon walls with lavender shadows
- teal rivers through rose cliffs
- mint grass under orange skies
- pastel snow against saturated blue mountains
- black glass volcanic ridges with aurora haze

Do not make every object equally loud.

Use calm scenic areas so weird landmarks can stand out.

### 7. Distant Landmarks Drive Exploration

Players should see places before they reach them.

Strong distant landmarks:

- mountain with a hole through it
- fossil spine across a canyon
- waterfall flowing upward
- glowing tree on a ridge
- tower on a far mesa
- floating island between peaks
- giant flower canopy
- crystal mountain reflecting a second sky

A good landmark should be describable from a distance.

### 8. Moving Curiosities Pull Attention

Every world should have at least one moving thing visible from far away.

Examples:

- circling signal light
- floating cloud whale
- walking flower
- ribbon creature
- orbiting relic
- prism bird
- crawling light on a fossil spine
- flock of glowing seeds

The moving curiosity is often the thing that makes a player say:

> “Look over here.”

### 9. Readable Weirdness

Weird things still need visual rules.

Use consistent signals:

- blue/cyan: traversal, air, water, climbable routes
- gold/yellow: objective, signal, high-value curiosity
- green/mint: living/reactive nature
- red/orange: danger, heat, storm, unstable behavior
- white/cream: carryable or neutral interactable

These are guidelines, not rigid UI rules.

### 10. Visual Comedy Is Welcome

The world can be beautiful and funny.

Examples:

- a giant sleeping hill-creature breathing
- a tiny machine worshipping a rock
- a trail sign pointing into the sky
- a flower that opens only when stared at
- a serious mountain with a ridiculous moving hat-like creature

Funny and beautiful is more memorable than merely beautiful.

## Real-World Inspiration Categories

Use real outdoor beauty as the emotional base:

- Yosemite granite cliffs
- Zion and Bryce canyon trails
- Sedona red rock shapes
- Banff turquoise lakes
- Swiss alpine ridges
- Patagonia mountain silhouettes
- New Zealand grassy mountains
- Icelandic volcanic valleys
- Pacific Northwest forests and waterfalls
- Olympic/Hoh rainforest
- Hawaiian volcanic slopes
- Utah arches and mesas

Do not copy specific places literally. Use them as landscape feelings.

## Avoid

Avoid:

- smooth empty planet terrain
- gray crater moons as the default
- generic No Man’s Sky alien sameness
- asset-store low-poly blandness
- full realism
- over-detailed textures
- visual clutter everywhere
- heavy fog that hides the world
- tiny test arenas pretending to be landscapes
- too many equal-saturation colors competing for attention

## Prototype Visual Direction

The first major visual target should shift from **Forbidden Moon** to a scenic impossible park.

Recommended first target:

# Rose Canyon Highlands

A warm canyon/highland park with:

- rose and peach canyon walls
- lavender shadows
- teal riverbeds or lowland trails
- distant mesas
- natural arches
- a climbable fossil spine
- switchback trails
- a moving signal creature above a ridge
- high overlook revealing the objective

This can preserve the existing antenna/storm/spine/entrance gameplay while changing the emotional read from:

> empty alien moon

to:

> impossible national park vista.
